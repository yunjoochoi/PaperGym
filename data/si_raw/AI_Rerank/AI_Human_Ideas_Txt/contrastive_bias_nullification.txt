Title: Contrastive Bias Nullification: Reducing Intersectional Biases in Large Language Models through Explicit Attribute Comparison

1. Problem Statement: Large language models often exhibit subtle biases that are difficult to detect and mitigate, particularly when these biases stem from complex interactions between multiple attributes. Current debiasing methods often focus on individual protected attributes or rely on predefined bias measures, which may miss more nuanced or intersectional biases.

2. Motivation: Existing debiasing methods typically struggle with intersectional biases due to their focus on individual protected attributes or use of predefined bias measures. These approaches may overlook subtle biases that emerge from the interaction of multiple characteristics. By explicitly contrasting different attribute combinations, we can assist the model in identifying and neutralizing biases that arise from the interaction of multiple characteristics, leading to more comprehensive debiasing. This method leverages the model's own capabilities to recognize and correct biases, potentially offering a more flexible and adaptable approach to bias mitigation.

3. Proposed Method: We propose Contrastive Bias Nullification (CBN), a prompting technique that generates pairs of contrasting scenarios that differ only in specific attributes (e.g., gender, race, age). The process involves the following steps:
    (1) Generate contrasting scenarios: Create pairs of scenarios that are identical except for specific attribute combinations.
    (2) Generate responses: Prompt the model to generate responses for each scenario in the pair.
    (3) Compare responses: Instruct the model to explicitly compare its responses, identifying any differences in tone, content, or implications.
    (4) Nullify bias: Prompt the model to rewrite its responses to eliminate these differences, effectively nullifying the bias.
    (5) Iterate: Repeat this process with different attribute combinations to address intersectional biases.
    (6) Meta-cognitive reflection: Include a step where the model reflects on its own biases and the challenges of maintaining neutrality.

4. Step-by-Step Experiment Plan:
    - Step 1: Dataset Preparation: Create a diverse dataset of prompts covering various domains (e.g., job applications, loan approvals, medical diagnoses) where intersectional biases might be present. Ensure the dataset includes a wide range of attribute combinations (gender, race, age, socioeconomic status, etc.).
    - Step 2: Baseline Evaluation: Evaluate the chosen language model's performance on the dataset using standard prompting techniques. Use existing bias metrics (e.g., regard scores, sentiment analysis) to establish a baseline level of bias.
    - Step 3: Implement CBN Prompting: Develop a prompting pipeline that implements the Contrastive Bias Nullification technique. This includes generating contrasting scenarios, prompting for responses, comparing responses, and instructing the model to nullify biases.
    - Step 4: CBN Evaluation: Apply the CBN technique to the same dataset used in the baseline evaluation. Measure the model's performance and bias levels using the same metrics as in Step 2.
    - Step 5: Comparative Analysis: Compare the results from Steps 2 and 4 to assess the effectiveness of CBN in reducing intersectional biases. Analyze both quantitative metrics and qualitative aspects of the generated responses.
    - Step 6: Ablation Studies: Conduct ablation studies to understand the impact of different components of CBN (e.g., removing the meta-cognitive reflection step, varying the number of contrasting scenarios).
    - Step 7: Generalization Tests: Test the CBN technique on new, unseen scenarios and attribute combinations to assess its generalization capabilities.
    - Step 8: Human Evaluation: Conduct a small-scale human evaluation to assess the perceived fairness and quality of responses generated using CBN compared to baseline responses.

6. Fallback Plan: If the Contrastive Bias Nullification method does not significantly reduce intersectional biases compared to baseline prompting, we can pivot the project in several ways. First, we could conduct a detailed error analysis to understand where and why the method fails. This might involve categorizing the types of biases that persist and analyzing whether they are related to specific attribute combinations or domains. We could then use these insights to refine our prompting technique or develop a hybrid approach that combines CBN with other debiasing methods. Additionally, we could expand our focus to analyze how the model's responses change across different iterations of the CBN process, potentially uncovering interesting patterns in how the model attempts to correct its own biases. This could lead to valuable insights into the model's internal representations of bias and fairness. Finally, we could explore the meta-cognitive aspect further, analyzing the model's reflections on its own biases to gain a deeper understanding of its capabilities and limitations in self-awareness and bias recognition. These alternative directions could still result in a valuable contribution to the field of AI ethics and bias mitigation in language models.
