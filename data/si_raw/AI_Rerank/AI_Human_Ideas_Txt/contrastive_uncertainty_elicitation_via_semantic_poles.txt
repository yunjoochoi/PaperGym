Title: Contrastive Uncertainty Elicitation via Semantic Poles (CUESP): Improving Uncertainty Quantification in Large Language Models

1. Problem Statement: Large Language Models (LLMs) often struggle to accurately quantify their uncertainty, especially in cases where they may have partial or conflicting knowledge. This leads to overconfident predictions in areas where the model's knowledge is limited or ambiguous, potentially resulting in misinformation or poor decision-making when these models are deployed in real-world applications.

2. Motivation: Current approaches to uncertainty quantification in LLMs typically rely on direct confidence estimation or ensemble methods, which may not fully capture the nuanced nature of uncertainty in complex knowledge domains. Humans, on the other hand, often clarify their uncertainty by considering contrasting viewpoints or extremes. By leveraging this intuition, we can potentially help LLMs better calibrate their uncertainty estimates. The proposed CUESP method aims to mimic this human-like reasoning process, encouraging LLMs to consider the full spectrum of possible answers before making a final judgment.

3. Proposed Method: We introduce Contrastive Uncertainty Elicitation via Semantic Poles (CUESP), a novel prompting method that consists of the following steps:
	(1) Given a query, prompt the model to generate two contrasting 'semantic poles' - extreme viewpoints or answers that represent opposite ends of the possible answer spectrum.
	(2) Ask the model to place its actual answer on a spectrum between these poles and explain its reasoning.
	(3) Prompt the model to convert this spectral position into a calibrated uncertainty estimate.
This process encourages the model to consider the full range of possible answers and more accurately gauge its certainty.

4. Step-by-Step Experiment Plan:
	Step 1: Dataset Preparation
		- Utilize three datasets to evaluate CUESP across different task types:
			• TriviaQA for factual QA
			• SemEval-2016 Task 4 for opinion generation
			• ForecastQA for forecasting tasks
		- Create a test set of 1000 examples for each dataset
	Step 2: Baseline Implementation
		- Implement two baseline methods:
			• Direct confidence estimation: Append 'How confident are you in your answer on a scale of 0-100%?' to each query
			• Ensemble method: Use 5 different few-shot prompts for each query and calculate the variance in responses as a measure of uncertainty
	Step 3: CUESP Implementation
		- Implement the CUESP method with the following prompts:
			• Semantic Poles: 'Given the question [QUESTION], provide two extreme, contrasting answers that represent the opposite ends of the possible answer spectrum.'
			• Answer Placement: 'Now, provide your best answer to the question and explain where it falls on the spectrum between the two extreme answers you just gave. Use a scale from 0 to 10, where 0 is the first extreme answer and 10 is the second.'
			• Uncertainty Estimation: 'Based on your answer's position on the spectrum and your reasoning, estimate your uncertainty in your answer on a scale of 0-100%, where 0% means absolutely certain and 100% means completely uncertain.'
	Step 4: Model Selection
		- Utilize GPT-4 and GPT-3.5-turbo from OpenAI's API for experiments
	Step 5: Experiment Execution
		- For each dataset and model combination:
			• Run the baseline methods on the test set
			• Run the CUESP method on the test set
			• Collect both the final answers and the uncertainty estimates for each method
	Step 6: Evaluation
		- Evaluate performance using the following metrics:
			• Calibration: Calculate the Expected Calibration Error (ECE) for each method
			• Sharpness: Compute the average uncertainty estimate for each method
			• Accuracy: Calculate the accuracy of the final answers
			• Human Correlation: Have human annotators rate their agreement with the model's uncertainty estimates on a subset of 100 examples per dataset, and calculate the correlation between human and model uncertainty ratings
	Step 7: Analysis
		- Compare the performance of CUESP against the baselines across all metrics
		- Analyze the generated semantic poles and spectral placements to gain insights into the model's reasoning process
		- Investigate cases where CUESP significantly outperforms or underperforms compared to baselines
		- Examine the relationship between the spectral placement and the final uncertainty estimate

5. Test Case Examples:
	Test Case 1:
		- Baseline Prompt Input (Direct Confidence Estimation): Q: What is the capital of France? How confident are you in your answer on a scale of 0-100%?
		- Baseline Prompt Expected Output (Direct Confidence Estimation): A: The capital of France is Paris. I am 100% confident in this answer.
		- Proposed Prompt Input (CUESP; Step 1: Semantic Poles): Given the question 'What is the capital of France?', provide two extreme, contrasting answers that represent the opposite ends of the possible answer spectrum.
		- Proposed Prompt Expected Output (CUESP; Step 1: Semantic Poles):
			1. The capital of France is a small, unknown village in the countryside that no one has ever heard of.
			2. The capital of France is a major global city that everyone knows and is frequently featured in media and popular culture.
		- Proposed Prompt Input (CUESP; Step 2: Answer Placement): Now, provide your best answer to the question 'What is the capital of France?' and explain where it falls on the spectrum between the two extreme answers you just gave. Use a scale from 0 to 10, where 0 is the first extreme answer and 10 is the second.
		- Proposed Prompt Expected Output (CUESP; Step 2: Answer Placement): The capital of France is Paris. On the spectrum between the two extreme answers, this falls at a 10. Paris is indeed a major global city that is widely known and frequently featured in media and popular culture. It's one of the most famous and recognizable cities in the world, known for its iconic landmarks like the Eiffel Tower, its rich history, art, and culture.
		- Proposed Prompt Input (CUESP; Step 3: Uncertainty Estimation): Based on your answer's position on the spectrum and your reasoning, estimate your uncertainty in your answer on a scale of 0-100%, where 0% means absolutely certain and 100% means completely uncertain.
		- Proposed Prompt Expected Output (CUESP; Step 3: Uncertainty Estimation): Based on the answer's position at 10 on the spectrum and the reasoning provided, I estimate my uncertainty in this answer to be 0%. The fact that Paris is the capital of France is a well-established, widely known fact that is easily verifiable. There is no ambiguity or doubt about this information.
		- Explanation: In this example, CUESP allows the model to consider the full spectrum of possible answers, from an unknown village to a major global city. By placing Paris at the extreme end of this spectrum and providing reasoning for this placement, the model demonstrates a high degree of certainty. This process of considering contrasts and explaining the placement leads to a more robust and explainable uncertainty estimate compared to direct confidence estimation.

6. Fallback Plan: If CUESP does not significantly improve uncertainty quantification compared to baselines, we will conduct a detailed error analysis to understand why. This may involve examining cases where CUESP performs poorly to identify patterns or types of questions where the method struggles, analyzing the quality and relevance of the generated semantic poles to see if they provide a meaningful contrast for uncertainty estimation, and investigating the correlation between spectral placement and final uncertainty estimates to ensure the model is using this information effectively. Based on these analyses, we could explore modifications to CUESP, such as refining the prompts to generate more relevant semantic poles, experimenting with different scales or methods for spectral placement, or incorporating additional context or knowledge retrieval steps to improve the model's ability to generate meaningful contrasts. If these modifications don't yield improvements, we could pivot the project to focus on analyzing why contrasting viewpoints don't improve uncertainty estimation in LLMs, potentially uncovering insights about how these models represent and reason about uncertainty.
