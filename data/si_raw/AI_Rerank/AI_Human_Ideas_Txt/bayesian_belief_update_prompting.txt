Title: Bayesian Belief Update Prompting: Improving Factual Consistency in Large Language Models

1. Problem Statement: Large language models often fail to appropriately update their beliefs when presented with new evidence, leading to inconsistent or outdated factual claims. This problem hinders the models' ability to maintain coherent and accurate knowledge representations across conversations or tasks.

2. Motivation: Existing approaches typically rely on retrieving and integrating external knowledge, but do not explicitly model the process of belief updating. By mimicking the process of Bayesian belief updating, we can guide the model to more rationally incorporate new information and maintain consistent beliefs over time. This approach leverages the model's inherent reasoning capabilities without requiring external knowledge bases or complex architectural changes.

3. Proposed Method: We propose Bayesian Belief Update Prompting, which consists of the following steps:
    (1) Elicit the model's prior belief about a statement.
    (2) Present new evidence.
    (3) Prompt the model to estimate the likelihood of observing this evidence given the statement is true versus false.
    (4) Guide the model to combine the prior and likelihood to form a posterior belief.
    (5) Ask the model to articulate its updated belief and explain the reasoning behind the update.
This process can be iterated with multiple pieces of evidence.

4. Step-by-Step Experiment Plan:
    - Step 1: Dataset Creation: Create a new dataset of multi-step fact-checking scenarios. Each scenario should consist of an initial statement, followed by 3-5 pieces of evidence that either support or contradict the statement. Include a mix of scenarios where the correct final belief should change or remain the same. Aim for 500 scenarios covering diverse topics.
    - Step 2: Baseline Methods: Implement two baseline methods:
        (1) Standard prompting: Simply ask the model to evaluate the statement after presenting all evidence.
        (2) Retrieval-augmented generation: Use a vector database to retrieve relevant information and include it in the prompt.
    - Step 3: Implement Bayesian Belief Update Prompting: For each scenario:
        (a) Prompt for prior belief: "What is your belief about [statement]? Express as a probability."
        (b) Present evidence: "New evidence: [evidence piece]"
        (c) Prompt for likelihood: "What is the likelihood of observing this evidence if the statement is true? What if it's false? Express as probabilities."
        (d) Guide posterior calculation: "Using Bayes' rule, calculate the updated belief."
        (e) Prompt for explanation: "Explain your updated belief and reasoning."
    - Step 4: Model Selection: Use GPT-4 and Claude-3.5 as the primary models for evaluation. Also test on open-source models like LLaMA-3 for comparison.
    - Step 5: Evaluation Metrics:
        (1) Accuracy: Compare final beliefs to ground truth.
        (2) Consistency: Measure belief changes in response to evidence.
        (3) Calibration: Assess how well expressed probabilities match actual correctness.
        (4) Reasoning quality: Human evaluation of explanation coherence and validity.
    - Step 6: Ablation Studies:
        (1) Vary the number of evidence pieces.
        (2) Test without explicit probability calculations.
        (3) Compare against a version that updates beliefs without Bayesian framework.
    - Step 7: Analysis: Examine scenarios where the method succeeds or fails. Categorize error types and identify potential improvements.

5. Test Case Examples:
    - Test Case 1:
        - Baseline Prompt Input: Statement: The Eiffel Tower was built in 1887. Evidence: 1) The Eiffel Tower was constructed for the 1889 World's Fair. 2) Construction of the tower began on January 28, 1887. 3) The tower was inaugurated on March 31, 1889. Is the original statement correct?
        - Baseline Prompt Expected Output: The original statement is partially correct but not entirely accurate. The Eiffel Tower was not built in 1887, but construction did begin in that year. Here's a more accurate statement: Construction of the Eiffel Tower began in 1887, and it was completed and inaugurated in 1889 for the World's Fair.
        - Proposed Prompt Input (Step 1: Prior Belief): Statement: The Eiffel Tower was built in 1887. What is your belief about this statement? Express as a probability.
        - Proposed Prompt Expected Output (Step 1: Prior Belief): Based on my knowledge, I would assign a probability of about 0.6 or 60% to this statement being true. The year 1887 sounds plausible for the construction of the Eiffel Tower, but I'm not entirely certain without additional information.
        - Proposed Prompt Input (Step 2: Present Evidence): New evidence: The Eiffel Tower was constructed for the 1889 World's Fair.
        - Proposed Prompt Input (Step 3: Likelihood Estimation): What is the likelihood of observing this evidence if the statement 'The Eiffel Tower was built in 1887' is true? What if it's false? Express as probabilities.
        - Proposed Prompt Expected Output (Step 3: Likelihood Estimation): Likelihood if true: 0.3 or 30%. If the Eiffel Tower was built in 1887, it's possible but somewhat unlikely that it would be constructed for an event two years later. Likelihood if false: 0.8 or 80%. If the statement is false, it's quite likely we'd see evidence about its actual purpose and timing.
        - Proposed Prompt Input (Step 4: Posterior Calculation): Using Bayes' rule, calculate the updated belief about the statement 'The Eiffel Tower was built in 1887'.
        - Proposed Prompt Expected Output (Step 4: Posterior Calculation): Let's apply Bayes' rule: P(True|Evidence) = P(Evidence|True) * P(True) / [P(Evidence|True) * P(True) + P(Evidence|False) * P(False)] = 0.3 * 0.6 / (0.3 * 0.6 + 0.8 * 0.4) ≈ 0.36 or 36% The updated belief that the Eiffel Tower was built in 1887 is approximately 36%.
        - Proposed Prompt Input (Step 5: Explanation): Explain your updated belief and reasoning about the statement 'The Eiffel Tower was built in 1887'.
        - Proposed Prompt Expected Output (Step 5: Explanation): My updated belief is that there's a 36% chance the Eiffel Tower was built in 1887. This is lower than my initial belief of 60%. The new evidence about the tower being constructed for the 1889 World's Fair makes it less likely that 1887 was the year of completion. While it's possible construction started in 1887 and finished in 1889, the original statement claiming it was 'built in 1887' seems less probable now. The evidence suggests the completion date was likely 1889 or very close to it, rather than 1887. However, without more specific information about the construction timeline, there's still some uncertainty, which is why the probability isn't zero.

6. Fallback Plan: If the proposed Bayesian Belief Update Prompting method does not significantly outperform baselines, we can pivot the project in several ways. We will conduct a detailed error analysis to understand where and why the method fails, involving categorizing error types and analyzing how they relate to the Bayesian update process. We will investigate how different prompting strategies for eliciting probabilities and likelihoods affect performance, experimenting with various phrasings, examples, or calibration techniques to improve the model's probability estimates. We may explore a hybrid approach that combines elements of Bayesian updating with retrieval-augmented generation, potentially leveraging the strengths of both methods. Additionally, we will analyze how the performance of the method varies across different types of facts or domains, which could provide insights into the model's underlying knowledge representation and updating mechanisms. If the Bayesian framework proves too complex for the model to handle reliably, we could simplify the approach to a more qualitative belief updating process while retaining the step-by-step nature of considering evidence sequentially.
