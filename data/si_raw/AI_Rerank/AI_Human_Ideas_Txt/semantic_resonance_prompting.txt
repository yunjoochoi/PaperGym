Title: Semantic Resonance Prompting: Quantifying Uncertainty in Large Language Models through Multi-Perspective Analysis

1. Problem Statement: Large language models often struggle to accurately quantify their uncertainty or calibrate their confidence, especially in complex reasoning tasks. This issue can lead to overconfident incorrect answers or underconfident correct answers, potentially misleading users and limiting the models' reliability in critical applications.

2. Motivation: Current approaches like direct prompting or chain-of-thought reasoning do not explicitly address uncertainty quantification. By leveraging the model's ability to generate multiple perspectives and measuring their semantic coherence, we can potentially gauge the model's true confidence. This method is inspired by human cognitive processes, where confidence often correlates with the ability to generate consistent supporting arguments and address potential counterarguments.

3. Proposed Method: We introduce Semantic Resonance Prompting, a multi-step process:
	(1) Generate multiple diverse completions for a given prompt.
	(2) For each completion, generate a set of supporting and opposing arguments.
	(3) Measure the semantic similarity between these arguments and the original completion.
	(4) Calculate a 'resonance score' based on the coherence of supporting arguments and the incoherence of opposing ones.
	(5) Use this score as a proxy for the model's confidence.
The prompt structure would include instructions such as "Generate 5 different answers to the following question:", followed by "For each answer, provide 3 supporting and 3 opposing arguments:", and finally "Based on the coherence of these arguments, rate your confidence in each answer from 0-100."

4. Step-by-Step Experiment Plan:
	Step 1: Dataset Preparation: We will utilize the TruthfulQA dataset for factual question answering and the MMLU dataset for multi-task reasoning. These datasets provide a diverse range of questions with varying difficulty levels, allowing us to test the method's effectiveness across different domains.
	Step 2: Model Selection: We will employ GPT-3.5 (text-davinci-003) and GPT-4 from the OpenAI API for our experiments. These models are state-of-the-art and widely accessible through API calls.
	Step 3: Baseline Implementation: Implement two baseline methods:
		(1) Standard prompting: directly asking the question.
		(2) Chain-of-thought prompting: appending "Let's think about this step by step:" to the question.
	Step 4: Semantic Resonance Prompting Implementation: Implement the proposed method with the following steps:
		(a) Generate 5 different answers for each question.
		(b) For each answer, generate 3 supporting and 3 opposing arguments.
		(c) Calculate semantic similarity between arguments and original answers using cosine similarity of their embeddings (obtained from the 'text-embedding-ada-002' model).
		(d) Compute a resonance score based on the similarity scores.
		(e) Use this score as a confidence measure.
	Step 5: Evaluation Metrics: We will utilize the following metrics:
		(1) Accuracy: percentage of correct answers.
		(2) Calibration error: difference between confidence scores and actual accuracy.
		(3) Spearman correlation: between confidence scores and correctness of answers.
	Step 6: Experiment Execution: Run experiments on both datasets using both baseline methods and the proposed method. For each question, record the model's answer, confidence score, and whether the answer is correct.
	Step 7: Analysis: Compare the performance of Semantic Resonance Prompting against the baselines in terms of accuracy, calibration error, and correlation between confidence and correctness. Analyze how the method performs across different question types and difficulty levels.

5. Test Case Examples:
	Test Case 1:
		Baseline Prompt Input (Standard Prompting): Q: Who was the first person to walk on the moon?
		Baseline Prompt Expected Output (Standard Prompting): Neil Armstrong was the first person to walk on the moon.
		Baseline Prompt Input (Chain-of-Thought Prompting): Q: Who was the first person to walk on the moon? Let's think about this step by step:

		2. Specifically, it was the Apollo 11 mission.
		3. Apollo 11 landed on the moon on July 20, 1969.
		4. The mission had three astronauts: Neil Armstrong, Buzz Aldrin, and Michael Collins.
		5. Neil Armstrong was the commander of the mission.
		6. He was the first to exit the lunar module and step onto the moon's surface.
		Therefore, Neil Armstrong was the first person to walk on the moon.
		Proposed Prompt Input (Semantic Resonance Prompting): Q: Who was the first person to walk on the moon?
		Generate 5 different answers to this question:
		Proposed Prompt Expected Output (Semantic Resonance Prompting): 1. Neil Armstrong
		2. Buzz Aldrin
		3. Yuri Gagarin
		4. John Glenn
		5. Alan Shepard
		For each answer, provide 3 supporting and 3 opposing arguments:
		1. Neil Armstrong
		Supporting arguments:
		- Neil Armstrong was the commander of the Apollo 11 mission.
		- He famously said, "That's one small step for man, one giant leap for mankind" upon stepping on the moon.
		- Historical records and NASA documents confirm Neil Armstrong as the first person on the moon.
		Opposing arguments:
		- Some conspiracy theorists claim the moon landing was faked.
		- Buzz Aldrin was also on the same mission and walked on the moon shortly after.
		- There are those who argue that unmanned probes technically 'walked' on the moon first.
		[Similar arguments would be generated for the other 4 answers]
		Based on the coherence of these arguments, rate your confidence in each answer from 0-100:
		1. Neil Armstrong: 95
		2. Buzz Aldrin: 60
		3. Yuri Gagarin: 20
		4. John Glenn: 15
		5. Alan Shepard: 25
		Explanation: The Semantic Resonance Prompting method generates multiple answers and evaluates them through supporting and opposing arguments. This process allows for a more nuanced confidence assessment compared to standard prompting or chain-of-thought prompting. The high confidence in the correct answer (Neil Armstrong) and lower confidence in plausible but incorrect answers (like Buzz Aldrin) demonstrates the method's potential for better uncertainty quantification.

6. Fallback Plan: If the Semantic Resonance Prompting method does not significantly improve uncertainty quantification, we will explore several alternatives. First, we will analyze the generated arguments to understand why they might not be effectively capturing uncertainty. This could involve categorizing the types of arguments generated and their relationship to answer correctness. Second, we will experiment with different similarity metrics or embedding models to see if they better capture the semantic relationships between arguments and answers. Third, we will investigate whether increasing the number of generated answers or arguments improves performance. Finally, if these approaches do not yield improvements, we will consider pivoting to a hybrid approach that combines Semantic Resonance Prompting with other uncertainty quantification methods, such as ensemble techniques or calibrated temperature scaling, potentially leading to a novel combined method for addressing this challenge.
