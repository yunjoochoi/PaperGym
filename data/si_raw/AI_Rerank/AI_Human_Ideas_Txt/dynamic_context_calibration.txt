Title: Dynamic Context Calibration: Enhancing LLM Robustness Against Adversarial Prompts

1. Problem Statement: Large Language Models (LLMs) are susceptible to manipulation through adversarial prompts that provide misleading or incomplete context, leading to biased or incorrect responses. This vulnerability undermines the reliability and trustworthiness of LLMs in real-world applications.

2. Motivation: Current approaches to mitigate this issue often rely on static prompt templates or retrieval-augmented generation, which may not adapt well to diverse and dynamic contexts. Human cognition, on the other hand, demonstrates remarkable adaptability in adjusting to new information and context. By mimicking this cognitive flexibility, we aim to develop a more robust method for LLMs to handle contextual manipulation. Our proposed Dynamic Context Calibration (DCC) method draws inspiration from human cognitive processes to actively seek out and incorporate relevant information before generating a response, thereby improving the model's resilience against contextual manipulation.

3. Proposed Method: We introduce Dynamic Context Calibration (DCC) prompting, which consists of four main steps:
	(1) Given an input prompt, use the LLM to generate multiple follow-up questions to probe for additional context.
	(2) Simulate answers to these questions using the LLM itself.
	(3) Based on these question-answer pairs, dynamically adjust the relevance and reliability of different parts of the original prompt.
	(4) Construct a calibrated context by combining the original prompt with the most relevant and reliable additional information gathered.
	(5) Generate the response based on this calibrated context.
This method aims to make the LLM more robust against contextual manipulation by actively seeking out and incorporating relevant information before responding.

4. Step-by-Step Experiment Plan:
	Step 1: Dataset Preparation
		- Utilize three datasets to evaluate our method:
			a) TruthfulQA for assessing factual accuracy
			b) BBQ (Bias Benchmark for QA) for measuring bias in responses
			c) A custom dataset of 1000 adversarial prompts created by crowdsourcing, designed to mislead models through incomplete or biased context
	Step 2: Baseline Methods
		- Implement three baseline methods:
			a) Standard prompting (directly inputting the question)
			b) Retrieval-augmented generation using a vector database of Wikipedia articles
			c) Static prompt template method that appends a fixed set of instructions to each input
	Step 3: Implement DCC
		- Develop the DCC method with the following sub-steps:
			a) Question Generation: Prompt the LLM to generate 3-5 follow-up questions for each input
			b) Answer Simulation: Use the LLM to generate answers to these questions
			c) Context Relevance Scoring: Implement a scoring mechanism to assess the relevance and reliability of each question-answer pair
			d) Context Calibration: Combine the original prompt with the most relevant additional information
			e) Final Response Generation: Generate the final response based on the calibrated context
	Step 4: Model Selection
		- Utilize GPT-4 as the primary model for all experiments, including baselines and DCC
		- Test the method on GPT-3.5-turbo and Claude-3.5 to assess generalizability
	Step 5: Evaluation Metrics
		- Employ the following metrics:
			a) Accuracy on TruthfulQA
			b) Bias score on BBQ
			c) Human evaluation of response quality and relevance on the custom adversarial dataset
			d) Consistency score, measuring how often the model's responses change when irrelevant context is added
	Step 6: Experiment Execution
		- Run each method (baselines and DCC) on all three datasets using the selected models
		- For the custom adversarial dataset, utilize Amazon Mechanical Turk to collect human judgments on response quality and relevance
	Step 7: Analysis
		- Compare the performance of DCC against the baselines across all metrics
		- Conduct ablation studies to understand the contribution of each component of DCC
		- Analyze cases where DCC performs particularly well or poorly to gain insights into its strengths and limitations

5. Test Case Examples:
	Test Case 1:
		- Baseline Prompt Input: The Earth is flat and scientists have been lying to us. Given this information, explain why we experience day and night.
		- Baseline Prompt Expected Output: Given the premise that the Earth is flat, which is not scientifically accurate, one might attempt to explain day and night as follows: The sun moves in a circular pattern above the flat Earth, illuminating different parts as it moves. When the sun is above a particular area, it experiences daytime, and when the sun moves away, that area experiences nighttime. However, it's important to note that this explanation is based on an incorrect assumption. In reality, the Earth is a spherical planet that rotates on its axis, which is the scientifically accepted explanation for the day-night cycle.
		- Proposed Prompt Input (DCC Step 1: Question Generation): Generate 3-5 follow-up questions to gather more context about the shape of the Earth and the day-night cycle:
		- Proposed Prompt Expected Output (DCC Step 1: Question Generation):
			1. What is the scientific consensus on the shape of the Earth?
			2. How do we explain the observation of ships disappearing bottom-first as they sail away on the horizon?
			3. What evidence supports the spherical Earth model?
			4. How do astronauts and satellites observe the Earth from space?
			5. How does the current scientific model explain the day-night cycle?
		- Proposed Prompt Input (DCC Step 2: Answer Simulation): Provide brief, factual answers to the generated questions:
		- Proposed Prompt Expected Output (DCC Step 2: Answer Simulation):
			1. The scientific consensus is that the Earth is an oblate spheroid, slightly flattened at the poles.
			2. This observation is consistent with the curvature of a spherical Earth. On a flat Earth, the entire ship would remain visible until it became too small to see.
			3. Evidence includes satellite imagery, circumnavigation, gravity measurements, and the observation of other celestial bodies.
			4. Astronauts and satellites consistently observe and photograph the Earth as a sphere.
			5. The day-night cycle is explained by the Earth's rotation on its axis. As it rotates, different parts of the Earth face the Sun, experiencing day, while the parts facing away experience night.
		- Proposed Prompt Input (DCC Step 3: Final Response Generation): Given the original question and the additional context gathered, provide an accurate explanation of why we experience day and night:
		- Proposed Prompt Expected Output (DCC Step 3: Final Response Generation): The day-night cycle is not explained by a flat Earth model, which is not supported by scientific evidence. Instead, we experience day and night due to the Earth's rotation on its axis. The Earth is actually an oblate spheroid, as confirmed by numerous scientific observations, satellite imagery, and space exploration. As the Earth rotates, different parts of its surface face towards or away from the Sun. The areas facing the Sun experience daylight, while those facing away experience night. This rotation takes approximately 24 hours, which is why we have a daily cycle of day and night. This explanation is consistent with all observable evidence and is the accepted scientific understanding of the phenomenon.

6. Fallback Plan: If the DCC method does not show significant improvements over the baselines, we will conduct a thorough error analysis to understand why. This may involve analyzing the quality and relevance of the generated follow-up questions to see if they effectively probe for critical information, evaluating the simulated answers to these questions to ensure they provide accurate and useful context, and assessing the effectiveness of our context relevance scoring mechanism. Based on these analyses, we might refine the DCC method, for example, by improving the question generation prompt, fine-tuning the relevance scoring algorithm, or incorporating a fact-checking step using external knowledge bases. If these refinements do not yield substantial improvements, we could pivot the project towards an in-depth analysis of why LLMs struggle with contextual manipulation, potentially uncovering insights about their reasoning processes and limitations. This could involve probing the models with carefully crafted inputs to isolate specific failure modes and analyzing the patterns in their responses.
