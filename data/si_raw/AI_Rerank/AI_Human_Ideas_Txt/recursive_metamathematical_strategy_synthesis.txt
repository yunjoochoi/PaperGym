Title: Recursive Metamathematical Strategy Synthesis for Improved Mathematical Problem Solving in Large Language Models

1. Problem Statement: Large language models often struggle to generalize mathematical problem-solving strategies to new, more complex problems. This limitation hinders their ability to tackle novel and challenging mathematical tasks, which is crucial for advancing AI capabilities in fields requiring advanced mathematical reasoning.

2. Motivation: Current methods typically rely on fixed sets of strategies or step-by-step reasoning, which may not capture higher-level mathematical thinking. Mathematicians often develop new strategies by combining and extending existing ones. By prompting the model to recursively synthesize and apply increasingly complex strategies, we aim to improve its ability to tackle novel, challenging problems. This approach mimics the way human mathematicians develop and apply new problem-solving techniques, potentially leading to more robust and generalizable mathematical reasoning in LLMs.

3. Proposed Method: We propose Recursive Metamathematical Strategy Synthesis (RMSS). RMSS starts with a set of basic mathematical strategies. For each problem, it prompts the model to: 
	(1) Identify relevant basic strategies
	(2) Synthesize a higher-level strategy by combining or extending these strategies
	(3) Apply the new strategy to the problem
	(4) Evaluate the effectiveness of the new strategy
	(5) Add the new strategy to the set if successful
This process is applied recursively, allowing for the creation of increasingly sophisticated strategies. The prompt includes explicit instructions for strategy synthesis, application, and evaluation.

4. Step-by-Step Experiment Plan:
	Step 1: Dataset Preparation: We will use the following datasets:
		• GSM8K for grade school math problems
		• MATH dataset for high school and college-level math problems
		• A subset of IMO (International Mathematical Olympiad) problems for extremely challenging tasks
	We will split each dataset into training, validation, and test sets.
	Step 2: Baseline Implementation: Implement the following baselines:
		• Standard prompting (direct question answering)
		• Chain-of-thought (CoT) prompting
		• Few-shot CoT prompting with 5 examples
		• Self-consistency with CoT (generate multiple CoT paths and take majority vote)
	Step 3: RMSS Implementation: Implement the RMSS method with the following steps:
		• Create an initial set of basic mathematical strategies (e.g., algebraic manipulation, geometric visualization, numerical estimation)
		• Design a prompt template for strategy identification, synthesis, application, and evaluation
		• Implement the recursive process of strategy generation and application
	Step 4: Model Selection: We will use GPT-4 as our primary model for all experiments. We will also test GPT-3.5-turbo and Claude-3.5 for comparison.
	Step 5: Experiment Execution: For each dataset and model combination:
		• Run all baseline methods and record their performance
		• Run RMSS method with varying depths of recursion (1, 2, and 3 levels)
		• Record the performance metrics (accuracy, solution quality, and runtime) for each method
	Step 6: Strategy Analysis: Analyze the strategies generated by RMSS:
		• Categorize the types of new strategies created
		• Evaluate the complexity and novelty of synthesized strategies
		• Assess the frequency of strategy reuse across different problems
	Step 7: Performance Evaluation: Compare the performance of RMSS against baselines:
		• Overall accuracy on each dataset
		• Performance on different difficulty levels
		• Ability to solve novel problem types not seen in training
	Step 8: Ablation Studies: Conduct ablation studies to understand the impact of different components:
		• Vary the initial set of basic strategies
		• Modify the strategy synthesis instructions
		• Alter the evaluation criteria for new strategies
	Step 9: Error Analysis: Perform detailed error analysis:
		• Identify common failure modes of RMSS
		• Compare error patterns between RMSS and baselines
		• Assess the quality of strategy synthesis in failed attempts
	Step 10: Generalization Test: Test the generalization capability of RMSS:
		• Apply strategies learned from one dataset to problems in another dataset
		• Evaluate performance on a set of handcrafted problems designed to require novel strategy combinations

5. Test Case Examples:
	Test Case 1:
		Baseline Prompt Input (Direct Prompting): Q: A factory produces 500 cars per day. If they increase production by 20% and work for 6 days, how many cars will they produce?
		Baseline Prompt Expected Output (Direct Prompting): To solve this problem, let's break it down step by step:
		1. Current production: 500 cars per day
		2. Increase in production: 20% = 0.2
		3. New production rate: 500 + (500 * 0.2) = 500 + 100 = 600 cars per day
		4. Number of working days: 6
		5. Total production: 600 * 6 = 3,600 cars
		Therefore, the factory will produce 3,600 cars in 6 days with the increased production rate.
		Proposed Prompt Input (RMSS; Step 1: Strategy Identification): Q: A factory produces 500 cars per day. If they increase production by 20% and work for 6 days, how many cars will they produce?
		Identify relevant basic strategies for solving this problem:
		Proposed Prompt Expected Output (RMSS; Step 1: Strategy Identification): 
		1. Percentage calculation
		2. Multiplication
		3. Step-by-step problem decomposition
		Proposed Prompt Input (RMSS; Step 2: Strategy Synthesis): Based on the identified strategies, synthesize a higher-level strategy for solving percentage increase problems efficiently:
		Proposed Prompt Expected Output (RMSS; Step 2: Strategy Synthesis): Percentage Increase Shortcut: Instead of calculating the increase separately and then adding it to the original amount, multiply the original amount by (1 + percentage increase) to get the new amount directly. This combines percentage calculation and multiplication into a single step.
		Proposed Prompt Input (RMSS; Step 3: Strategy Application): Apply the synthesized Percentage Increase Shortcut strategy to solve the problem:
		Proposed Prompt Expected Output (RMSS; Step 3: Strategy Application): 
		1. Original production: 500 cars per day
		2. Percentage increase: 20% = 0.2
		3. New production rate: 500 * (1 + 0.2) = 500 * 1.2 = 600 cars per day
		4. Total production for 6 days: 600 * 6 = 3,600 cars
		The factory will produce 3,600 cars in 6 days with the increased production rate.
		Proposed Prompt Input (RMSS; Step 4: Strategy Evaluation): Evaluate the effectiveness of the Percentage Increase Shortcut strategy compared to the basic step-by-step approach:
		Proposed Prompt Expected Output (RMSS; Step 4: Strategy Evaluation): The Percentage Increase Shortcut strategy is more efficient than the basic step-by-step approach:
		1. It reduces the number of calculations from 3 (increase, addition, multiplication) to 2 (single multiplication, then multiplication by days).
		2. It's less prone to intermediate rounding errors.
		3. It's more generalizable to problems with different percentage increases.
		4. It's faster to apply, especially in mental calculations.
		Given these advantages, the Percentage Increase Shortcut strategy should be added to our set of problem-solving strategies for future use.
		Explanation: The RMSS approach demonstrates the ability to identify relevant basic strategies, synthesize a higher-level strategy (Percentage Increase Shortcut), apply it effectively to solve the problem, and evaluate its effectiveness. This process leads to a more efficient solution method that can be applied to similar problems in the future, showcasing the potential for recursive improvement in problem-solving strategies.

6. Fallback Plan: If the proposed RMSS method does not significantly outperform the baselines, we will conduct a thorough analysis to understand the limitations. This may include examining the quality and relevance of synthesized strategies to identify potential improvements in the synthesis process, analyzing the types of problems where RMSS underperforms to understand its limitations and potentially develop specialized strategies for these cases, investigating the impact of different initial strategy sets on the performance of RMSS, exploring alternative prompting techniques for strategy synthesis and application, and considering a hybrid approach that combines RMSS with other methods like retrieval-augmented generation or self-consistency. Additionally, we could pivot the project towards an in-depth analysis of the strategies generated by RMSS, focusing on their novelty, complexity, and potential insights they might offer into the reasoning capabilities of large language models in mathematical domains.
